10 things I wish someone told me, when I started investing

"If I knew then what I know now,
I'd be a billionaire."



Be a boring investor!

The tortoise will always beat the hare. I see so many people now-a-days trying to get rich overnight. The recent GameStop and AMC fiasco is a perfect example! In the large majority of circumstances, buying into individual companies is more risky than it is worth. I am sure that you have heard, "Don't put all your eggs in one basket" or "You have to diversify your investments," these statements are very true! I think that the safest and most rewarding way to invest is to mostly stick to buying something called ETFs. An exchange traded fund (ETF) is a type of product that involves a collection of securities, such as stocks. ETFs have their own tickers just like individual companies, so you can buy them just as you would an individual company. ETFs are often meant to mimic an index, like the S&P 500, or a sector, like the energy sector. So when you buy Vanguard's Energy Index Fund ETF (VDE), you are buying a collection of stocks from the energy sector all in one fund!! INSTANT DIVERSIFICATION! Now, ETFs are very boring. They aren't typically going move like some individual companies do, but they are safe!

Be a lazy investor!

Investing is hard work! Sitting there watching tickers go up and down can be especially stressful. I have had a situation before where I was watching a ticker for a company I had just invested in, and I was not totally sold on it. Sitting there, watching that ticker go up and down every second made me go crazy. I panic sold that company. A few months later, and a solid earnings report, that company when up 20%! Had I been a lazy investor, like I am now, I would not have psych'd myself out and dumped it for no good reason. Now, I am not saying that you should not pay attention to your investments but you need to be reasonable about how you watch them. Checking the price of your investment every 2 seconds is not going to make the price go up. Now-a-days, I only check my investments once a day, after the market closes. This allows me to avoid all of the useless, meaningless moves that stocks partake in throughout the trading day, and focus on the only thing that actually matters, which is what the price settles on going into closing.

Do not be afraid to lose some on the way up.

A company is not going to make you rich over night. In fact, it might go down 5-10% before it reaches it's full potential. Be very patient. If you invest in a fund or a company that you truly believe in, and have researched for a while now, then you have nothing to worry about. If a company has a great balance sheet, have wonderful prospects for the future, and is projected to grow in the coming years, then it's price WILL go up. Short term loses are nothing compared to the larger growth. In the summer of 2020, I had invested in a company called Micron Technology (MU), which is part of a larger computer micro-chip and memory sector. The computer mmicro-chip and memory sector was undoubtedly one of the fastest growing sectors in the US stock market for 2020, but for whatever reason, despite having great fundamentals and good growth prospects, Micron Technology's price was trading flat for the whole summer. Other memory device maker's were going up 2-4% pretty regularly, but Micron Technology was lagging behind at a price point around $50. I did not really understand why, but I kept my faith in that company because I knew that on paper it was a great company. Micron Technology did eventually catch up with it's sector, and man am I glad that I stuck with it.

Remember, the market will always open the next business day.

If you are down a couple of percent on the day, do not worry! You are not going to make money every day. Red days do happen, and there is nothing you can do about it. If it is 3pm, and you notice that you are down quite a lot, do not scramble and execute random, off the wall, trades in an attempt to make all your money back. First of all, that is not possible, and second you are going to end up losing yourself even more money, digging your hole even deeper. I am guilty of doing it before, as I am sure most everyone who trades is. It is a natural urge. When you find yourself in a situation like that, take a step back and remind yourself that this is not the last day that the stock market is in session, It is going to be open the next business day, and the day after that, and the day after that and so on.. There will always be oportunities to make money in the future, just be patient. Remain composed, have faith, and remember the market will always open the next day, unless it's Friday of course..

Learn about compound interest.

Compound interest and time are your bestfriends when it comes to wealth building. If you invest a certain amount and put that money in safe and boring ETFs, that offers a solid return, that money will grow exponentially. For example, at age 25, if you put 100$ perweek into investments that give you a 11% return annually (Which is the bare minimum return in today's market), you will have 1.2 million dollars by age 55. It works like this, if you have 10,000 in an ETF that averages 7% growth per year, after a year you will have 10,700. After the second year, that 7% is now working to grow the original investment, 10,000, plus, the new 700 that was added last year. So instead of making 700 in year one, you will make 749 in year two, 801 in year three, 857 in year four, and 4,980 in year 30, to bring the grand total to 76,123. So by sticking 10,000 into a boring ETF that gives you 7% growth per year and ignoring it for 30 years, you have grown your original investment by 760%! Oh and by the way, most ETFs do not give you 7% growth per year, it is very easy to find ETFs that give you much much more.

You do not need a lot of money to start investing.

I know that whenever you think about investing, you think of millionaires on yachts throwing money on beautiful people that they paid to be there. Ugh, but The Wolf of Wall Street is a great movie though. The truth is, I personally know of several great funds that you can buy into right now for only 40$ that will give you a double digit percentage return each year. you do not need to be rich to make your money work for you. First of all, 86% of people in American do not invest anything into the stock market, and the large majority of the people that do, only invest extra money that they save each month off their income. Do not feel shameful or insignificant for only investing a small amount. A little money invested is better than no money invested at all. When I first started investing, I only invested like $200. $200 is way more than $0.

Do not be afraid of stock market crashes

Before I started investing, I always looked at the S&P's previous years, and said to myself, "Now I know, the moment I start investing, the stock market is going to crash." I always thought the the market's best years are behind it and I missed the boat. The market will not do as well for me. Here is a piece of advice, the best time to start investing was 10 years ago, the second best time is right now. Stock market "crashes" are far from the end of the world, and in fact, a pullback in overall prices of securities is healthy for the market to keep going. In January of 2020, imagine that you put $1,000 into the company Apple. In March of 2020, news of Covid-19 breaks out, and the stock market "crashes", and drops by 33%. Your apple investment is now worth about $700. If you kept your cool, and did not pull your money out in March of 2020, you would have made the 33% (you originally lost) back by May of 2020, and by February of 2021 you would have doubled your original investment to make that 1,000 turn into 2,000. A lot of people assume that when the stock market "crashes" you immediately lose all of your money, this is not the case. A stock market "crash" just means that the prices of companies in the stock market dip by a large percentage, suddenly. That is what happened in the 1930s, in 2007, and in 2020. Do not sell when a crash happens, just wait patiently and you will get your money back shortly and then some.

Do not listen to the Chicken Littles.

Stock market blogs and forums are absolutely saturated with people who claim to know that the US stock market is doomed, and a crash is imminent. I heard it 4 years ago, 2 years ago, a year ago.. Guess what? The stock market is still pumping, it is still hitting record levels, as it has been for the last 150 years. Only three things in life are gauranteed: Death, taxes, and the Dow Jones Industrial Average reaching record highs this year. Ever since I started trading I have read posts of people preaching doom and gloom naratives about the stock market. "The sky is falling! The sky is falling!" I am not blaming these people, it is natural to worry about money. Money makes the world go round. I am just saying that in my experience, 100% of the time these people were wrong and the US economy has stuck around, and it shows no signs of going anywhere anytime soon.

There is no perfect strategy to investing

Stock trading is a math game about consistently putting yourself in situations where you stand to make a good rate of return, and avoid situations where there is a high probability that you will lose money. That is it. There is no secret sauce. There is not a list of instructions on how to invest properly. the way that I invest is different from the way my neighbor invests, which is different from the way that his neighbor invests. If everyone invested the same way, the market would not function. I am saying all of this to get across the point that when you watch YouTube videos of people claiming to know how to make a lot of money it is not true. If everyone is doing it, it is not special. Take even what I say strictly as advice. At the end of the day, do your own research and educate yourself.

Do not let other people talk you into investing into certain products

If your friend tells you to invest into a certain company and that company starts to go down a little bit, your friend is going to have a lot more conviction in holding that investment, and waiting out the bad days than you will. You see, he knows that company a lot better than you do. He knows about the recent earnings reports, and about the CEO's outlook on the future. To you, this is just some random company that your friend convinced you to invest in. You have no intellectual or emotional tie to that company. You are way more likely to sell than he is. On the flip side, if you invest into a company or fund that you have researched and are really knowledgable about, you are more likely to hold it and reap all of it's reward! Stick to what you know. Besides, it is way more rewarding to research into a company, analyze it's recent activities, and carefully invest into it and watch it grow. In that case, not only are you making money but you are also watching all of your research come to fruition and be validated.



Investing Resources

Do not be afraid to get help, and educate yourself about what is happening in the stock market, and never assume that you know exactly what is happening. The stock market is an ever-evolving entity. Below, I am going to give three links to websites that I personally use to stay up to date on my investments.

- The first one is Yahoo Finance. Yahoo Finance is terrific at gathering all of the information you need to know about your company and putting it in one place. This site has financial data, Yahoo Finance's analyst's rating on whether you should buy that company or not, and it also includes a comment section for Yahoo Finance's users to discuss the company with other investers.

- The second one is SEC.gov. the securities and exchange commision is the location that you can find all of your company's current, and past, filings, which is very helpful to know.

- The third is Nasdaq. This website is similar to Yahoo Finance, but the one thing that I am recommending this site for is that they gather press releases that your company makes, and lists them in a tab next to your company. So, consider Nasdaq as a complimentary website to Yahoo Finance.

What does that mean?
Index

Not tradable. An index is a subset of the stock market that helps investors compare current price levels with past prices to calculate market performance. If your personal return percentage is higher than say the S&P 500's (a standard index) return percentage then you are doing better than average.

Individual Company

An individual company is a company that is publically tradeable. For example, Walmart, Home Depot, Amazon...

Sector

Sector is a broad term that groups businesses that buy and sell similar goods. Individual companies are in direct competition with each other. For example, There is the Energy sector, Healthcare sector, Technology sector..

Ticker

A ticker is price of a certain company or fund. Tickers are updated continuously throughout a trading session.

Market Hours

You can buy and sell investments during market hours. The stock market opens at 9:30am EST and closes 4:00pm EST, Monday through Friday, except for on holidays.

Balance Sheet

A company's balance sheet is a document that a company has that represents it's overall financial health. It's assets, liabilities, how much cash the company has on hand..

Percent Return

When I talk about a certain percent return per year, I am referring to price growth and dividend yield. Price growth is when the price of your investment goes up. So if you invest in an ETF at $20, and it's price goes up to $25, that a 25% return. Some companies offer dividend payouts. This is called a 'yield' percentage. Companies that do offer a dividend, offer it around 1-2% a year. That company will pay you 1-2% of the money you have invested into it per year. Usually, dividends get payed quarterly.

Security

A security is a broad term that describes something that you invest into. For example, a security could be a stock, an ETF, a mutual fund, a CD, a savings account..

A Stock Market "Crash"

A crash has happened when there is a sudden, unexpected turn in the overall stock market. There is not a universally agreed upon percentage of decline for a downturn to be considered a "crash". In March of 2020, the overall stock market crashed by 30%.

Earnings Reports

All publically traded companies in the United States are required by law to file earnings reports quarterly and annually. This includes how much money they gained or lossed for that time period.

Product

A product is generally the same thing as a "security", a financial instrument that you can invest in, to hold value or produce income.
